Key messages

Problem identification

COVID-19 is only amplifying what we’ve always known; that safe, stable housing is part of the foundation that creates health and wellness. When that foundation becomes shaky,   

  • whether it’s from not being able to afford monthly payments or utilities 

  • whether it’s from being stuck at home with family members with unresolved/ unaddressed trauma who may inflict violence 

  • whether it’s from being unhoused or having to live in crowded settings like nursing homes, shelters, or prisons 

  • or whether it’s from living in a racially or economically segregated neighborhood 

...the instability of this foundation is affecting our physical and mental health, as well as other support structures we need to thrive.  

Impact statement

In this health note we’re going to talk more fully about how and why housing is impacting our resilience through COVID-19, why it is contributing to health inequities, and what that means for us. 

When we think of healthy housing, we may initially think of the quality of the physical structure someone lives in. But housing is so much more than the sum of a shelter’s construction materials. 

For example, does someone lives in a home with  

  • overcrowded living conditions? 

  • poor ventilation?  

  • Extreme temperatures or water and energy leaks?  

  • asthma triggers like mold, pests? and   

  • environmental contaminants like lead or tobacco smoke? 

If so, they may be more likely to experience chronic and respiratory conditions like asthma, cancer, cardiovascular disease, and COPD. 

Features of substandard housing quality, such as excessive moisture and dampness, inadequate or poorly maintained heating and ventilation systems, crowding, pest infestations, deteriorated carpeting, and structural defects are associated with lowered immunity and exposure to respiratory infections. 

In addition to housing quality, stable housing is an important factor in health equity because being without stable housing can be detrimental to one’s health. Not only do people who are chronically homeless face lower life expectancy and higher morbidity in physical and mental health. People who experience housing insecurity are more likely to experience poor health in comparison to their stably housed peers. 

For example, when someone  

  • falls behind on rent,  

  • needs to move frequently (particularly if they needed to move 2 or more times a year ~ considered “extremely housing insecure”) 

  • needs to temporarily live in others’ homes or a shelter, or  

  • lives in overcrowded housing conditions 

...it can increase chronic stress, which can suppress the effects of the immune system. Additionally, housing instability can result in disruptions to employment, social support networks, education, proper storage of medications, and the receipt of social service benefits. 

Residential instability like eviction or foreclosures can lead to an increase in depression, anxiety, and alcohol use. It is also associated with health problems among youth, including increased risks of teen pregnancy, early substance use, and depressive symptoms.  

Residential crowding has also been linked to both psychological distress and physical illness (for example, the spread of infectious disease). 

According to MHC’s 2019 housing report, approximately 23 percent of homeowners with a mortgage and approximately 45 percent of renter households in Louisville spent more than 30 percent of their income on housing between 2000 and 2017. Some families were even “severely cost-burdened,” meaning they spent more than 50 percent of their income on housing. The “cost burden” associated with having to spend most of your income on housing often leaves little money for other living expenses, like food and utilities, let alone access to other health-related amenities such as educational expenses, recreational programs, or healthcare. Much of this burden is more likely to fall upon renters rather than homeowners. 

This means that families who experience the financial strain of having difficulty paying mortgages or rent are more likely to postpone medical treatment when they need it. Nationally, severely cost-burdened renters are 23 percent more likely than those with less severe burdens to face difficulty purchasing food. Homeowners who are behind in their mortgage payments are also more likely to lack a sufficient supply of food and to go without prescribed medications, compared to those who do not fall behind on payments. 

Researchers have found that the availability of resources such as public transportation to one’s job, grocery stores with nutritious foods, and safe spaces to exercise are all correlated with improved health outcomes. Neighborhood characteristics such as living in residentially segregated areas or near high-volume roads, however, can be a danger to health and can result in increased rates of respiratory diseases such as asthma and bronchitis and increased use of health care. 

Because of Louisville’s history of redlining, in which the federal government made it legal for banks and insurance companies to deny black families access to loans, devalue their homes, and forced them to live in segregated neighborhoods; today over 75% of the Louisville’s Black population lives on less than 5% of the land. Because that land isn’t within proximity of living wage jobs or nutritious food, and is more likely to be located near industries that pollute the land, water, and air, we see many inequitable health outcomes in market areas that correspond with the Louisville’s racially and economically segregated areas.

Recommendations

What can we do? 

To start, there are 3 different actions we can all take today. 

1) Address the root cause of health outcomes 

2) Understand how the systems of power (e.g. racism, classism, ableism) have shaped these root causes 

3) Work together to develop interventions that repair the harm created by segregation policies and institutional practices 

 

What do we mean by “Address the root cause of health outcomes?” 

If you’re comfortable with doing so, I want to paint a picture for you.  

Imagine a tree.  

When we see trees, we usually judge its health by those leaves you’re picturing. Whether the leaves are full or few. This is similar to our own health as people, we’re judged by the health outcomes we experience. That’s everything from diabetes to drug use. This is why we say it’s helpful to think about our health outcomes as the leaves in the tree.  

But, we know that our health does not start with those outcomes. Just like the health of a tree doesn’t start with those leaves. So, if we want to understand the health of the people we serve, we need to understand the roots. And for people, those are called root causes to our health. This includes access to healthy, stable, and affordable housing, access to living wage jobs and transportation, food access, etc.  

What we know is that we see patterns in health outcomes across Jefferson County because we see patterns in how people experience root causes. So, as public health folks, we have to ask a question. Why? Why do we see patterns in how people experience root causes? Because we all experience them. Right? We all experience housing, schools, or the health care system. But, how and how well we do so shapes what our health looks like.  

So why do we see differences in the way people experience housing stability, the resources in their neighborhoods, or in housing quality? 

That brings us to our second point. 

Understand how the systems of power ‘in the soil’ (e.g. racism, classism, ableism) shaped housing and these root causes 

So we like to take this metaphor a step further and say the soil that the tree is planted in shapes the conditions of the roots and the eventual leaves. In this metaphor, the soil are all of our systems of power.  

Power is the ability to achieve a purpose. Systems of power impact the design of and experience with root causes, including what how it is resourced and what it is resourced to do.  

We can see power through strategies like agenda setting, mobilizing around an action, and influencing perspective and world view. Whether or not power is good or bad depends upon the purpose. 

A person can:  

  • exercise a sense of agency by voting or participating in the political process 

  • hold positional power over another or choose to redistribute that power to work WITH others 

  • serve as a gatekeeper on behalf of an institution to determine whether a recipient gets access to a resource 

  • or benefit from a system of policies and institutional practices in a way that’s inequitable or in a way that benefits everyone 

Systems of power can be equitable or inequitable. Inequitable systems of power are the -isms and –phobias. In the context of housing in this country, systems of racism, classism, and ableism have dramatically impacted people’s health, opportunities, and their quality of life.  

For example racism, or assigning value to a person based upon their racial phenotype, was used to create racially and economically segregated neighborhoods. Even today, classism is used to concentrate wealth and keep low income families out of affluent neighborhoods. Additionally, ableism is used to design structures that don’t include occupants with health conditions (like seniors or people with disabilities) in a meaningful way when they try to interact with the environment.  

Rejecting the abuse of these systems means that we must unlearn inequitable behaviors or else we'll get in our own way and make it much harder to ensure everyone has what they need. 

We know we have to think critically about power during this COVID-19 time. Whether it’s being used by property managers to raise rents during the eviction moratorium or by utility companies to suspend shutoffs, this is an important time to consider power since the old normal is gone and by working together we get the opportunity to build a better world.  

So, if we think about power and how abusive power dynamics have shaped where we are today, we must mobilize and use power-building strategies to reimagine a different future. CoVid has already shown us that it is possible to do what we’ve been told cannot be done. 

Explainer

Purpose

Stable, quality housing is a key indicator of health. Where someone lives can affect the health of our families, our access to resources like food, education, jobs, and our sense of wellbeing. Healthy housing is often determined by factors such as housing stability, housing quality, housing affordability, and their neighborhood characteristics. Healthy housing means that the housing is built in a way that is structurally sound and free of environmental toxins, a stable and consistent source of shelter for the person(s) living there, costs less than 30% of a person’s income, and is within proximity of amenities like quality jobs, food, and schools that provides families the tools they need to thrive. 

Even before the spread of CoVid-19, access to (guaranteed) healthy housing is needed for individual health, but also the health of the entire community. Making sure that the people making low wages have safe, stable housing reduces our health care costs and generally improves the mental health and wellness of our community.   

Even though we know this discussion is about housing & health, COVID-19 is teaching us lessons about where the cracks are in our system regarding housing policy, resourcing, and availability.  

Health impacts

We know that older adults and people of any age who have serious underlying medical conditions are at higher risk for severe illness if they contract COVID-19. Particularly people: 

    • with chronic lung disease or moderate to severe asthma  

    • who have serious heart conditions  

    • who are immunocompromised  

Families who have to choose between the cost of housing, food, and utilities are more likely to develop underlying conditions such as: 

  • diabetes  

  • chronic kidney disease (in which they have to undergo dialysis)  

  • liver disease

How is this related to CoVid-19?

As a result of the historical inequities that occurred before the outbreak of CoVid-19, Louisville’s black residents are more likely to live in neighborhoods with a lack of healthy food options, amenities, recreational facilities, lighting, and safety.  

 

Due to segregated neighborhoods and racially segmented labor markets, black residents in Louisville are more likely to:  

  • have to commute outside of their neighborhoods for work or access to food and other amenities, 

  • be public transit users,   

  • work in jobs where they can’t miss work or work from home, 

  • work at or below the median income,   

  • be uninsured or underinsured. 

 

They are also more likely to live in deteriorating housing stock or near polluting industries. 

Not only are black residents more likely to be represented in essential worker positions (e.g. healthcare, transportation, food service, sanitation, etc.) that make it harder to participate in social distancing, residential segregation contributes greatly to many of the underlying conditions that complicate treatment for residents from those areas who contract CoVid-19. 

How do we work together to repair harm?

At the department of Public Health and Wellness, we address health equity by leveraging 

    • Data 

    • Community-Centered Engagement 

    • Strategic Partnerships 

    • Policy Level Interventions 

At LMPHW, we work to improve the health of the citizens of Louisville by providing individuals, groups, and communities with the tools to make informed decisions about their well-being. We use data to inform our decision-making so that we can make equitable choices about where our priorities lie. 

For example, when we look at our 2017 Health Equity Report (HER), our data shows how many of the underlying conditions that complicate CoVid-19 correspond with the market areas that have been racially and economically segregated through redlining. We know that these are the areas with older and deteriorating housing stock and that the differences in health outcomes are caused not only by historic segregation policies but often continues because of the housing and land use practices that we see today. 

Because civic engagement and democratic decision-making are important factors for improving community health, we rely upon community engagement strategies like Healthy Louisville 2025 to work WITH communities and provide those most impacted by health inequities the opportunity to engage in decision-making activities that directly benefit their own health, which is how the health department became more involved in the connection between health and housing. 

In order to create an experience for residents, that is equitable and addresses the root cause of the inequities they experience, we rely upon strategic partnerships to understand what creates these inequities. Strategic Partnerships also ensure that we include the right people at the table as we work together to repair these systemic harms. 

We use this information to inform policy level decisions to ensure that we are protecting the health and wellness of our local residents. 

So what types of policies should we be advocating for? That’s where you come in.  

Healthy Louisville 2025 is about working together to create a community health improvement plan that reimagines what’s possible for an equitable future. 

Whether it’s advocating for moratoriums on evictions, renter protections, or to fund more affordable or shared equity housing in Louisville, it is up to us to work together to ensure families have the resources they need to lead healthy lives. 

Being aware of how power creates inequities in housing means that in addition to safe, stable, and affordable housing we should be advocating for policies like living wages and paid sick leave or universal health care for essential workers, because it’s all connected to healthy housing.  

 

Recap: Why is healthy housing important? 

We know that housing is an important root cause if we ever expect to have good health. We know that housing stability, affordability, quality and access to amenities must be considered in the treatment and prevention of CoVid-19. We also know that in order to achieve health equity, access to healthy and affordable housing must be given to all communities.
